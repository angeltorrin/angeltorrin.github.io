03 — How I Work

From insight to execution, without losing either.

Every project I take on follows the same starting point, regardless of the sector or the scale.

I start with the business, not the brief. Before I touch a campaign I want to understand what the organisation is actually trying to achieve commercially, where the gaps are and what the people closest to customers are seeing that the data does not always show.

Then I go to the market. Competitors, consumer behaviour, category trends, what is working elsewhere and why. I look for the gaps between what the business thinks its audience wants and what that audience actually does. Only then do I build the strategy. And when I build it, I build it to be executed, not admired. Plans that cannot be delivered are not strategies. They are wish lists.

The result is marketing that works at both ends: clear enough to get buy-in, specific enough to get results.

STORY 01

Choose Pharmacy First

The problem

In January 2024 the NHS launched Pharmacy First, letting pharmacists treat seven common conditions like sore throat, earache and UTIs at the pharmacy instead of waiting days for a GP. It was worth £7.4 million to the business, but the real problem was patient understanding. A service only exists if people know they can use it, and most patients still defaulted to their GP for conditions a pharmacist could treat that day.

It was not one campaign either. Each country in the UK offered different services under Pharmacy First, so a single national message would have been wrong somewhere, and in healthcare, telling a patient they can get a treatment their local pharmacy does not offer is worse than saying nothing.

The solution

I planned and executed the national marketing across all 750 stores. That started with understanding exactly what was available in each nation, then building the campaign so every region carried the right message for the services its pharmacies could actually deliver, with the point of sale and patient materials versioned accordingly.

The thinking behind every decision was the patient. The message had to be simple enough that people genuinely understood they could come to us, and the materials had to work for every community our stores served, not just the easiest ones to reach.

The results

Measurable increases in service uptake across the estate, and pressure taken off GP appointments, which was the whole point of the NHS programme. Every region ran with messaging accurate to the services available there.

In healthcare marketing, clarity is a patient outcome. If a patient does not understand a service exists, they do not get treated. It shaped how I communicate complex propositions everywhere since: lead with what it means for the person, not the organisation.

STORY 02

The mobile health clinic

The problem

Access to healthcare should not depend on where you live, but it does. Some people cannot get to a pharmacy: no transport, no branch nearby, or lives that simply make it hard. Those are often the people who need a blood pressure check or a vaccination the most, and they were the people our stores were never going to reach by waiting.

The solution

Well Pharmacy ran a mobile health clinic, a renovated van we took out to different locations, offering flu and COVID vaccinations and blood pressure checks. I ran the marketing for it: helping decide where it went based on need, areas of deprivation, places without a nearby pharmacy, or locations that just made access easy, and promoting it locally so people knew it was coming. Separately, for Know Your Numbers month, we partnered with Kinetik Wellbeing on a dedicated blood pressure event.

Instead of waiting for people to come to us, we took the service to them. Every decision, from the locations to the messaging, started with the same question: who is missing out and how do we reach them.

The results

Vaccinations and blood pressure checks were delivered directly into communities that needed them most, to people who otherwise might not have been seen at all. Some of those blood pressure checks will have caught something someone did not know they had.

Access is a marketing problem as much as a service problem. The services existed. The difference was whether the right people knew about them and could actually reach them. I bring that lens to every healthcare brief: what does the patient need to hear, not what does the business want to say.

STORY 03

Two weeks to capture a competitor's patients

The problem

Two of the UK's largest pharmacy chains started closing stores nationally. Their patients needed a new pharmacy and they were going to choose one quickly. Whoever moved first would win them, and being second was worth nothing.

The solution

I saw the window. I mapped the closing stores against our nearest locations, looked at the patient volumes those sites were handling, built the case for a fast targeted campaign, and in two weeks we designed, produced and deployed door drops and in-store materials across the relevant locations.

The results

Patient registrations increased at every targeted store. One of the fastest and most commercially effective campaigns we ran.

The best opportunities sit in the gap between what everyone else is watching and what is actually happening. Speed matters as much as the idea. I have kept that opportunistic radar on ever since, including at the agency, where spotting a client's competitor going quiet became a trigger to push harder, not coast.

A Dubai luxury real estate brokerage

The problem

Dubai real estate is one of the most crowded markets in the world, and every brokerage in it calls itself luxury, so the word had stopped meaning anything. The client was a newer name going up against established players, with a brand that did not yet look or sound as premium as the clients it wanted, low organic visibility on a young website, and no real separation between its two very different audiences: public sales, and private, off market listings.

Their actual edge, discreet access to exclusive off market properties, was buried. The brief was to make them credible, visible and consistently on brand, and to turn that into qualified leads for both buyers and private listings.

The solution

I started with the brand, because in luxury, consistency is credibility. I refined their brand guidelines so every touchpoint, website, social, listing decks and messaging, looked and sounded like one premium brand, and sharpened the positioning away from generic luxury toward what actually set them apart: personal, discreet service and access to properties most buyers never see.

Then I built the marketing on a clear set of pillars, so the content had a job to do rather than posting for the sake of it:

  • Location authority: area and community guides and developer spotlights, so they owned the conversation around where to buy.
  • Market intelligence: price trends, investment insight and market news, to earn trust with serious buyers and investors.
  • The private collection: the exclusive, off market listings, positioned as the reason to come to them rather than anyone else.
  • Lifestyle: the aspirational side of luxury Dubai living, to build the brand and feed social.

I split the two markets so each had its own approach. Public sales ran on visibility and volume: SEO, paid search and social, with developer led content to capture active buyers. Private sales ran on discretion and relationships: quieter, targeted outreach and positioning that made them the broker high net worth sellers would trust with an off market listing.

On search, I worked closely with our SEO specialist to rebuild the website's visibility. We mapped the keywords that actually signalled intent, specific communities, developers and property types, fixed the technical issues holding the site back, restructured the location and developer pages, and tied a content plan to the marketing pillars so the site began building real authority instead of sitting invisible behind bigger names.

None of this was a solo effort, and my job was to make it one coherent whole. I owned the brief and the client relationship, and I led the team delivering it: the social media team running content and community across the channels, the SEO specialist, designers, content writers, videographer and paid media. I set the direction, briefed each discipline against the pillars, and kept everything on time, on brand and pointed at the same goals.

The social team mattered more than usual here. In luxury property, social is where credibility is built long before an enquiry lands, so I worked closely with them to make sure the feed carried the brand's new positioning, the lifestyle and private collection pillars had a consistent voice, and every post looked like it belonged to a broker worth trusting with an off-market listing.

How we tracked it

Performance was tracked through Meta Business Suite and GA4, with Search Console for organic visibility. We measured against a 90 day baseline taken before the restructure, then compared the following 90 days using the same audience parameters and the prior equivalent window as the benchmark, so the comparison was like for like rather than seasonal noise. Leads were split by source and by market, buyer enquiries and private listing enquiries tracked separately, so we could see which pillar was doing the work.

The results

Over the two matched 90 day windows, restructuring the content strategy and campaign approach drove up to a 35% increase in engagement and a 20% uplift in conversion rates. Organic visibility climbed for the luxury community and developer keywords that mattered, and qualified enquiries grew across both funnels: a steadier flow of buyer leads from search, paid and social, and a stronger, better positioned pipeline for private, off market listings.

Most importantly, they started to be taken seriously as a high end name, which was the whole point. Brand, content, search and paid pulled into one strategy, and a team pulled in one direction.
